DEFINITION Partial Airway Obstruction – Difficulty breathing but still able to move air/ventilate. Causes could include: • Foreign Body • Infection (croup or epiglottitis), or • Anaphylaxis. For Anaphylaxis, refer to EMS Policy \#726 - Adult Treatment Protocols - Allergic Reaction and Anaphylaxis Shock. Severe or Complete Airway Obstruction – Poor air exchange, silent cough, gasping, cyanosis, increased difficulty breathing, unable to ventilate. II. BLS TREATMENT VITALS Assess lung sounds, does the patient have the ability to move air. OXYGEN Use as needed. Ventilate patient / use airway adjuncts as needed / avoid oral airways when suspected infection (stridor and severe difficulty breathing). ABDOMINAL If patient is awake and has complete obstruction perform abdominal thrusts THRUSTS until object dislodges or the patient becomes unresponsive. CPR If patient is unresponsive perform chest compressions checking the patients mouth regularly for obstruction. DO NOT PERFORM a blind finger sweep. III. ALS TREATMENT MONITOR Treat rhythm as appropriate. VASCULAR ACCESS Appropriate for patients’ condition. SEVERE / COMPLETE AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION MAGILL’S FORCEPS If unresponsive, perform direct laryngoscopy and remove obstruction with Subject: ADULT TREATMENT PROTOCOLS - AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION Policy Number 712 Page 2 of 2 STRIVING FOR EXCELLENCE Magill forceps if applicable. Once obstruction is removed use appropriate airway as needed. NEEDLE If unable to ventilate patient, consider air transport or transport patient to CRICOHYROTOMY closest Emergency Department PARTIAL OBSTRUCTION / INFECTION EPINEPHRINE Consider 1.0 mg of 1:1000 nebulized in 5 ml of normal saline for severe stridor and extreme difficulty breathing.
